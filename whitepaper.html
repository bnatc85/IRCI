1. Executive Summary

IRCI—the Investor Relations Composite Index—is the first quantitative framework that converts investor relations quality into a single, peer-benchmarked score and translates every point into enterprise value dollars.

Public companies invest millions annually in investor relations, yet IR teams have long lacked a unified, defensible metric to demonstrate the financial impact of their work. IRCI solves this by synthesizing four research-backed performance dimensions—Valuation, Liquidity, Coverage, and Trust—into a composite score on a 0–100 peer-relative scale.

Each IRCI point carries a dollar value derived from academic research (Bushee & Miller, 2012), scaled by company enterprise value and statistical model fit. For a $135 billion company, a single IRCI point can represent over $33 million in enterprise value.

Beyond scoring, IRCI provides an AI-powered playbook of 20+ prioritized actions, predictive analytics for forecasting future scores, budget optimization via quantum-ready algorithms, and board-grade PDF reports—all from a single analysis run.

2. The Problem: IR's Measurement Gap

Investor relations sits at the intersection of finance, communications, and strategy. Despite its strategic importance, the discipline suffers from a fundamental measurement problem:

No Unified Metric

IR teams track dozens of disparate KPIs—stock price performance, analyst coverage counts, trading volume, media mentions, earnings call attendance, institutional ownership percentages—but no single, composite benchmark ties them together into a holistic view of IR quality. This fragmentation makes it impossible to answer the simple question: "How good is our IR program?"

No Dollar-Value Attribution

When CFOs and boards ask "What is investor relations worth to this company?", IR professionals struggle to provide a defensible, dollar-denominated answer. Without a quantitative link between IR activities and enterprise value, IR budgets are perpetually vulnerable to cost-cutting exercises.

No Peer Benchmarking Standard

A company may know its own media mention count or institutional ownership percentage, but without peer-relative benchmarking, these numbers lack context. Is 45% institutional ownership good or bad? It depends entirely on the peer group—and currently, no standard framework provides this comparison.

No Evidence-Based Playbook

Even when gaps are identified qualitatively, IR teams lack a systematic, evidence-backed methodology for prioritizing which actions will have the greatest impact on measurable outcomes. Recommendations are typically anecdotal rather than data-driven.

The Core Insight: IR creates value through four observable channels—valuation multiples, trading liquidity, media/analyst coverage, and investor sentiment. IRCI is the first system to measure all four simultaneously, score them against peers, and convert the result into dollars.

All scoring uses median-anchored percentile scaling, which maps each metric to a 0–100 scale where the peer-group median equals 50. This approach is robust to outliers, handles small peer groups gracefully through range compression, and provides intuitive interpretation: above 50 means above-median, below 50 means below-median.

Default Dial Weights

Valuation 35%
Liquidity 35%
Coverage 15%
Trust 15%

These defaults can be auto-optimized using ridge-regularized non-negative least squares (NNLS) regression to find weights that maximize the correlation (R²) between IRCI scores and enterprise values within the peer group.

4. The Four Dials

4.1 Valuation Dial (35% Default Weight)

The Valuation dial measures how the market values a company relative to its fundamentals. Academic research consistently shows that effective investor relations reduces information asymmetry, which directly improves valuation multiples (Bushee & Miller, 2012).

Primary Metric: EV/EBITDA (70% of dial)

Enterprise Value to EBITDA is the workhorse valuation multiple used by professional investors. IRCI computes this ratio for each company in the peer group and converts it to a median-anchored percentile where lower EV/EBITDA ratios (indicating relative undervaluation by the market) receive higher scores.

Secondary Metric: PEG Ratio (30% of dial)

The Price/Earnings-to-Growth ratio adjusts the P/E multiple for growth expectations, sourced from Yahoo Finance and Alpha Vantage. When unavailable, the dial relies entirely on EV/EBITDA.

Valuation Dial Formula Valuation = 0.70 × percentile(EV/EBITDA, lower_is_better) + 0.30 × percentile(PEG, lower_is_better) Valuation Gap = (company_EV/EBITDA - peer_mean) / peer_mean × 100%

4.2 Liquidity Dial (35% Default Weight)

The Liquidity dial measures how easily a company's stock trades without moving the price. Strong IR programs attract institutional investors and market makers, tightening spreads and increasing turnover. This dial carries equal weight to Valuation because liquidity directly impacts a company's cost of capital and ability to raise funds.

Component Metrics

Key Liquidity Formulas Amihud = |daily_return| / dollar_volume (Amihud, 2002) Roll Spread = 2 × √(max(-autocovariance, 0)) (Roll, 1984) Turnover = dollar_volume / market_cap Institutional % = 13F holdings / shares outstanding

Daily metrics are computed over the quarter, with Amihud and Roll spread values winsorized to handle outliers. The quarterly aggregate uses the median for Amihud and Roll spread (robust to extreme days) and the mean for turnover.

4.4 Trust & Sentiment Dial (15% Default Weight)

The Trust dial measures market confidence and perception—how investors and media perceive the company's communication and transparency. It is the most AI-intensive dial, leveraging FinBERT transformer models for financial sentiment analysis.

Component Metrics

MetricWeightDescription
Event Calmness40%Volatility around SEC filing events (Fama-French 5-factor residuals)
Media Tone30%FinBERT sentiment of news articles, with reliability shrinkage
Social Sentiment15%Reddit rank changes, StockTwits bullish ratio
Baseline Calmness15%Quarterly stock return volatility
Event Calmness For each SEC filing event: event_volatility = Σ|residual_returns| over ±3 trading days residuals from Fama-French 5-factor model (or CAPM fallback) event_calmness = -median(event_volatilities) Interpretation: Lower volatility around events = calmer market reaction = higher trust
FinBERT Media Tone with Reliability Shrinkage raw_score = P(positive) - P(negative) per article raw_tone = mean(article_scores) media_tone = raw_tone × shrinkage_factor shrinkage = n_articles / (n_articles + 4) With 2 articles: shrinkage = 0.33 (high uncertainty, shrink toward 0) With 20 articles: shrinkage = 0.83 (high confidence, trust the signal)
Why Shrinkage Matters: A company with only 2 articles—both positive—should not receive the same high sentiment score as a company with 50 positive articles. The Bayesian shrinkage factor pulls small-sample estimates toward neutral, preventing noise from dominating the signal.

5. Composite Score Calculation

IRCI Composite Score IRCI = (Valuation × 0.35) + (Liquidity × 0.35) + (Coverage × 0.15) + (Trust × 0.15) Result: 0–100 peer-relative percentile scale • 75–100: Strong (top quartile) • 50–75: Above median • 25–50: Below median • 0–25: Attention needed (bottom quartile)

Median-Anchored Percentile Scaling

Rather than simple min-max normalization, IRCI uses a median-anchored approach that provides several advantages:

Scaling Algorithm if value ≤ median: percentile = (value - min) / (median - min) × 50 else: percentile = 50 + (value - median) / (max - median) × 50
  • Median = 50 always: The middle of the peer group is always anchored at 50, making scores intuitive.
  • Outlier-robust: Extreme values at the top or bottom compress naturally rather than distorting the entire scale.
  • Small-group compression: For peer groups of 2–5 companies, the range is compressed (e.g., 15–85 for 3 peers) to avoid giving false precision to a narrow comparison.

Weight Auto-Optimization

Users can override default weights or run the auto-optimizer, which uses ridge-regularized non-negative least squares regression:

Auto-Optimization Objective: maximize R² of regression EV ~ IRCI minimize: ||Xw - y||² + λ||w||² subject to: w ≥ 0, Σw = 1 λ = 0.05 (ridge penalty) Cross-validation: 3-fold rolling CV

6. Dollar Value Attribution

The single most powerful feature of IRCI is its ability to assign a dollar value to each point of the composite score—enabling IR teams to speak the language of the C-suite.

Methodology

The dollar attribution is grounded in the academic finding by Bushee & Miller (2012) that investor relations programs contribute approximately 5–10% to firm enterprise value over the long term. IRCI operationalizes this as follows:

Dollar Value Per IRCI Point $/Point = Enterprise Value × 0.05% × R² Derivation: • Total IR contribution to EV: ~5% (conservative end of 5–10% range) • Typical IRCI gap between leader and laggard: ~50 points • Per-point impact: 5% ÷ 50 = 0.10% • Conservative halving: 0.10% ÷ 2 = 0.05% • Scaled by R² (model fit quality) for statistical defensibility

Worked Example

Large-Cap Example — $135 Billion Enterprise Value, R² = 0.50

$/Point = $135B × 0.0005 × 0.50 = $33.75 million per IRCI point

• A successful press release (+0.75 IRCI pts): +$25.3M implied value
• An investor day (+3.2 pts): +$108M implied value
• A comprehensive IR program overhaul (+8 pts): +$270M implied value

Quarterly IR Contribution

Quarterly Attribution Quarterly IR Contribution = (Current Q IRCI - Previous Q IRCI) × $/Point × Impact Factor Default Impact Factor: 10% Rationale: The cross-sectional regression measures structural (multi-year) differences. A single quarter captures only marginal, incremental improvement. The 10% factor converts structural $/point into a conservative quarterly estimate.

The impact factor is user-adjustable on a 1–100% slider, allowing teams to model conservative (5%), moderate (10%), or aggressive (25%) attribution scenarios for board presentations.

8. AI & Machine Learning Components

IRCI uses AI as a precision enhancement to a quantitative finance framework—not as its foundation. This is a critical distinction: the core scoring engine is deterministic and reproducible, while AI adds three layers of intelligence on top.

8.1 FinBERT Sentiment Analysis

FinBERT is a BERT transformer model fine-tuned on 50,000+ financial documents. Unlike general-purpose sentiment analyzers, it understands that "the company beat expectations by cutting workforce" contains both positive and negative financial signals. IRCI applies FinBERT to every news article collected for the Trust dial's media tone component.

8.2 AI Chat Assistant

A multi-provider conversational AI (Claude, GPT, Gemini) allows users to ask natural-language questions about their results. The assistant is context-aware, meaning it has access to the specific IRCI scores, peer comparisons, and raw metrics for the current analysis. Example: "Why is our liquidity score below the peer median?" yields a data-driven explanation referencing the company's specific Amihud ratio vs. peers.

8.3 AI-Generated Playbook

For each analysis, IRCI generates 20+ prioritized recommendations using large language models. Each recommendation includes:

  • Priority level (High / Medium / Low) based on dial gaps
  • Specific action description with implementation details
  • Expected IRCI impact (point estimate and timeframe)
  • Academic citations supporting the recommendation
  • Key metrics to track for measuring success
  • Recommended tools and platforms
  • Industry benchmarks for context

8.4 Predictive Analytics

Machine learning models forecast next-quarter IRCI scores based on historical trends, planned IR initiatives, and seasonal patterns. Users can model "what-if" scenarios: "If we host an investor day and increase press release cadence, what's the projected IRCI impact?"

Key Differentiator: AI components in IRCI are enhancers, not the engine. Remove the AI layers and you still have a fully functional, deterministic quantitative scoring system. Add them back and you get richer insights. This is the opposite of generic LLM tools, where removing the AI leaves nothing.

11. Statistical Rigor & Validation

IRCI is designed to meet the evidentiary standards of finance professionals and academic researchers. Every output includes supporting statistical metrics.

R-Squared (R²)

The coefficient of determination measures how much variance in enterprise value is explained by IRCI scores within the peer group. Reported with every analysis, R² directly scales the dollar attribution—ensuring that weak correlations produce conservatively small dollar estimates.

P-Value

Statistical significance of the IRCI-to-EV relationship. Only relationships with p < 0.05 are flagged as statistically significant, ensuring users don't act on noise.

Peer Count Validation

IRCI warns when peer groups are too small for reliable statistical inference. Range compression for small groups (2–5 companies) prevents false precision, and analyses with fewer than 3 peers carry explicit uncertainty caveats.

Fama-French Risk Adjustment

The Trust dial's event calmness metric uses Fama-French 5-factor residuals—the gold standard in academic finance—to isolate company-specific volatility from market-wide, size, value, profitability, and investment factors. This prevents confounding: a calm market day isn't mistakenly credited to good IR.

Winsorization & Outlier Handling

Daily liquidity metrics are winsorized to the 1st/99th percentiles before aggregation, preventing a single anomalous trading day from distorting quarterly scores.

13. Academic Foundations

IRCI's methodology rests on a substantial body of peer-reviewed finance and communications research. Key supporting studies include:

Bushee & Miller (2012)

"The Role of the Business Press as an Information Intermediary." Journal of Accounting Research. Demonstrated that proactive IR programs contribute 5–10% to firm enterprise value by reducing information asymmetry between management and investors. This finding anchors IRCI's dollar attribution methodology.

Kirk & Vincent (2014)

"Professional Investor Relations within the Firm." The Accounting Review. Found that companies with dedicated IR professionals experience lower bid-ask spreads, higher institutional ownership, and greater analyst coverage—validating the Liquidity and Coverage dial constructs.

Amihud (2002)

"Illiquidity and Stock Returns: Cross-Section and Time-Series Effects." Journal of Financial Markets. Introduced the Amihud illiquidity ratio (|return|/volume), which became the standard measure of price impact and forms the primary component of IRCI's Liquidity dial.

Roll (1984)

"A Simple Implicit Measure of the Effective Bid-Ask Spread in an Efficient Market." Journal of Finance. Developed the Roll spread estimator from return autocovariance, used in IRCI's Liquidity dial as a proxy for bid-ask spreads when direct spread data is unavailable.

Fama & French (2015)

"A Five-Factor Model of Expected Stock Returns." Journal of Financial Economics. Provided the five-factor risk model used in IRCI's Trust dial to compute residual returns—isolating company-specific volatility from systematic market factors.

Huang, Teoh & Zhang (2014)

"Tone Management." The Accounting Review. Demonstrated that linguistic tone in financial disclosures predicts future firm performance, supporting the inclusion of FinBERT sentiment analysis in the Trust dial.
